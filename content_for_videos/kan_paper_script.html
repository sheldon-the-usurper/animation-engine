KAN: Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks

01
Hook
The Question Nobody Was Asking
0:00 – 1:30
▶
Visual: Open on a blank screen. Slowly type: "What if the most important piece of every AI system was wrong?" Then cut to your face.

Every large language model. Every image generator. Every recommendation algorithm. At their core, they all share one thing: a structure called a Multi-Layer Perceptron — or MLP for short.

MLPs have been around since the 1980s. They're so fundamental that most researchers don't even question them anymore. They're just... the default.

But in April 2024, a team from MIT published a paper asking: what if we've been doing this wrong the whole time? What if there's a better building block — one that's not just more accurate, but one you can actually look inside and understand?

That paper is called KAN: Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks. And today we're going to break down exactly what it does, why it matters, and honestly — where it falls short too.

02
Background
What Even Is an MLP? (The Honest Explanation)
1:30 – 5:00
▶
Visual: Draw a simple 3-layer neural network on screen as you explain. Circles connected by lines. Label the circles "neurons" and the lines "weights."

Before we get to KAN, we need to make sure everyone's on the same page about MLPs — because a lot of explainers skip this and then the rest doesn't make sense.

Imagine a neural network as a series of layers. Each layer has nodes — we call them neurons. Information flows from left to right. Each neuron takes in numbers, does a little calculation, and passes a number to the next layer.

Think of it like a factory assembly line. Raw materials go in on the left. Each station transforms the material slightly. At the end, you get a finished product. Each "station" is a layer of neurons.

Now — and this is the key thing to understand — there are two parts to what each connection does in an MLP:

First, there's a weight: a number that says "how much do I scale this signal?" Think of it like a volume knob. Second, there's an activation function: a fixed mathematical shape — like ReLU, which just means "if negative, make it zero; if positive, keep it." The activation function lives on the neuron (the node itself).

So in an MLP: the weights are learned during training, but the activation functions are fixed — you choose them before training starts, and they never change.

This is the exact thing KAN challenges: why should activation functions be fixed?

The justification for MLPs comes from something called the Universal Approximation Theorem, which basically says: given enough neurons, an MLP can approximate any function. So MLPs are theoretically capable of anything.

But "theoretically capable" and "practically efficient" are very different things. And this is where the paper starts making its case.

03
The Math Origin
The 70-Year-Old Theorem That Started This
5:00 – 8:30
▶
Visual: Show portraits of Kolmogorov and Arnold. Then show the theorem equation — but don't dwell on it. Use it as a visual prop, not an explanation device.

Here's where things get interesting. KAN is inspired by a mathematical result from the 1950s called the Kolmogorov-Arnold Representation Theorem.

Two Russian mathematicians — Andrey Kolmogorov and Vladimir Arnold — proved something profound: any complex multi-variable function can be broken down into a combination of simpler single-variable functions, just added together.

Here's an analogy. Imagine you're trying to describe the price of a house, which depends on many things: square footage, location, number of rooms, age. The theorem says: you don't need a wildly complex multi-variable formula. You can always find individual "shape functions" — one for square footage, one for location, etc. — and just add their outputs together to get the price. It's always possible to decompose it this way.

Now, this theorem was discovered in the 1950s. And people immediately tried to use it for machine learning. But it failed. Here's why: the theorem guaranteed these decomposition functions exist, but it said nothing about them being smooth. In the worst case, they could be fractal — infinitely jagged — which no computer can learn.

So for decades, this theorem was considered "theoretically beautiful, practically useless." The machine learning community moved on and built MLPs instead.

But the MIT team had a different take. They said: look, most real-world functions — in physics, in biology, in science — are smooth. The nightmare fractal case is a mathematician's worst-case scenario, not what you actually encounter in practice. And they asked: what if we just built a network around this theorem anyway, for smooth functions, and trained it with modern techniques like backpropagation?

That's KAN.

04
Core Idea
How KAN Actually Works
8:30 – 12:30
▶
Visual: Use the MLP vs KAN architecture diagram from your prep. Point to nodes vs edges explicitly.

Okay, let's get into the actual architecture. The change KAN makes sounds almost too simple:

In an MLP, activation functions sit on the nodes — the neurons — and they're fixed. The edges (connections) just multiply by a weight number.

In a KAN, this is flipped. The nodes do nothing special — they just add up what they receive. The activation functions sit on the edges, and crucially, they are learnable. Every single connection learns its own custom shape function during training.

If an MLP is like a road network where every car follows the same speed limit sign posted at each intersection — KAN is a network where every single road segment has its own smart speed controller that learns the optimal behavior for that specific route.

These learnable edge functions are represented as splines. A spline is a smooth curve made of pieced-together polynomial segments — like those smooth bezier curves in graphic design tools. You've seen them. The key property of splines is that you can adjust them locally: you change one part without messing up the whole curve. They're precise and flexible.

▶
Visual: Show a simple spline curve being adjusted — drag one control point and show how the rest stays smooth. This visual is very effective.

So instead of: input → multiply by weight → apply fixed ReLU → next layer...

KAN does: input → pass through a learned spline function → output → sum at node → next layer.

There are literally no linear weight matrices in a KAN. Every single parameter is part of a spline function on an edge.

This is the key intuition: KAN is MLPs on the outside, splines on the inside. It inherits the compositional learning ability of MLPs, plus the precision of splines for fitting individual functions.

And here's the elegant result of this structure: because each edge is learning its own shape, when training is done, you can literally look at the network and see what mathematical relationship each connection learned. You can visualize the whole thing. That's the interpretability claim — and it's a real one.

Property MLP KAN
Activation functions Fixed, on nodes (e.g. ReLU) Learnable splines, on edges
Weight parameters Scalar numbers Entire 1D functions
Nodes do Apply activation + sum Just sum inputs
Interpretable? Hard — need post-hoc tools Yes — visualize each edge
Inspired by Universal Approx. Theorem Kolmogorov-Arnold Theorem

The Numbers
What the Benchmarks Actually Show
12:30 – 16:00
▶
Visual: Use the 4-chart benchmark panel from your prep graphics. Walk through each one individually.

Now let's talk numbers — because this is where papers either live or die.

The headline result is on solving PDEs — partial differential equations. These are equations that describe how physical things change in space and time: heat flow, fluid dynamics, wave propagation. They're notoriously hard to solve numerically.

100×
More accurate on PDE solvingKAN achieves 10⁻⁷ MSE vs MLP's 10⁻⁵. Same task, dramatically different precision.
100×
More parameter efficientKAN used 100 parameters. MLP used 10,000. For the same level of performance.
∝ N⁻⁴
Better scaling lawKAN's error drops as N to the power of negative 4 as you add params. MLP's is much shallower. KAN gets more efficient faster.

Now — and I want to be really clear about this because the paper is honest about it — these results are on scientific function fitting tasks. Small, structured problems where there's a clean underlying mathematical relationship. The kind of thing physicists deal with.

This is not "KAN beats GPT-4." The paper is not claiming that. The domain is AI + Science, and that's where these numbers hold.

There's also a significant catch: KAN trains much slower than MLP. Because every edge is a whole spline function instead of a single number, training is roughly 10 times slower with the original implementation. The community has since built FastKAN, which gets this down to about 2× slower — still a real cost.

06
The Cool Part
KAN as a Scientific Discovery Tool
16:00 – 18:30
▶
Visual: Show a simplified visualization of a trained KAN — a small network where the edge curves are drawn as actual function shapes. This is from the paper's own figures.

This is my favorite part of the paper, and I think it's genuinely exciting for science.

After training a KAN, you can look at it. You can see what each edge learned. And because the functions are smooth splines, you can then ask: does this shape look like a known function? Does it look like a sine wave? A logarithm? An exponential?

The paper demonstrates this with two real examples. In one, they train a KAN on data from knot theory — a branch of mathematics — and the network independently rediscovers known mathematical relationships between knot invariants. Relationships that mathematicians had discovered through years of work.

In another, they use it on data from Anderson localization — a quantum physics phenomenon — and the network learns to represent the physical relationship accurately, in a form that a physicist can inspect and recognize.

It's like the difference between a black box that gives you an answer, and a colleague who shows you their work. KAN doesn't just predict — it shows you the mathematical skeleton it found in the data. For scientists who are trying to discover equations, not just make predictions, that's transformative.

The authors call KAN a "collaborator" for scientists. I think that's exactly the right framing. This is not about replacing deep learning for language or vision. It's about giving researchers a new kind of tool for understanding quantitative data.

07
Honest Take
Where KAN Falls Short
18:30 – 20:30

Okay, let's be honest — because the hype around this paper when it dropped was enormous, and not all of it was warranted.

A follow-up paper called "KAN or MLP: A Fairer Comparison" ran controlled experiments — same parameter count, same compute budget — across real ML tasks. The conclusion: KAN only clearly beats MLP on symbolic formula representation. On vision tasks, NLP tasks, audio tasks — MLP wins or ties.

Other researchers found KAN struggles with noisy data and irregular functions. The spline representation, which is great for smooth scientific functions, doesn't handle noise as robustly as MLPs with dropout.

And the training speed problem is real. Even with FastKAN, you're paying a cost that makes scaling to large models expensive.

So: KAN is not an MLP killer. It is a specialized tool, and a genuinely excellent one, for structured scientific data where interpretability matters and the underlying functions are smooth. That's a real and valuable niche. But don't expect to see a GPT built on KANs anytime soon.

08
Outro
What Comes Next + The Ecosystem
20:30 – 22:00
▶
Visual: Show a simple tree/ecosystem diagram: pykan at the root, branching into FastKAN, ChebyKAN, GraphKAN, WavKAN, Vision-KAN.

The community response to this paper was remarkable. Within weeks, developers had built a whole ecosystem around it.

There's FastKAN and FasterKAN — optimized implementations that are 2× slower than MLP instead of 10×. There's ChebyKAN, which replaces splines with Chebyshev polynomials for even better mathematical properties. There's GraphKAN for graph neural networks, Vision-KAN for image models, and WavKAN using wavelet functions.

The original code is open-source — pip install pykan — and it's worth playing with if you work with scientific data. It's genuinely fun to train a small KAN and watch it visually learn a mathematical relationship.

The deeper story here is about the tension at the heart of deep learning: accuracy versus understanding. MLPs are powerful but opaque. KANs trade some of that power for transparency — and in the right domain, that transparency is worth more than raw performance.
